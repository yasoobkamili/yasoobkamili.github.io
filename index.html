Dumail, Naranaag

11 July 2021
The trek begins from Naranag village, situated at around 2277 meters above sea level. A few hundred meters into the trail, we pass by ancient Hindu temples, offering a quick historical stop. After approximately 4 km and 2 hours of trekking, Dumail is reached at ~2400 m ASL. The trail up to Dumail features minimal elevation gain and is rated as easy. The name “Dumail” is derived from “Du” (two) and “Mail” (join), marking the confluence of two rivers—Marchoi Nala and another stream originating from Gangbal Lake. These form the Wangath River. A distinctive wooden bridge at Dumail marks the crossing point to the next section of the trail. From Dumail, the trail transitions into a moderate gradient ascent through alpine meadows for the next 5 km. This section involves a steady climb with scenic views of the surrounding forested ridgelines and open pastures. After approximately 4 additional hours of trekking, the Harmukh peaks come into view, signaling proximity to Marchoi Valley, which sits at an altitude of about 2850 m.
Trek Grade: Easy to Moderate
Total Distance: 19 kms

Rainuer, The Endless Meadow

11 July 2021
The trek begins from Hirpora village in Shopian at approx 2385 meters above sea level. Trekking in this area usually requires prior permission due to its proximity to sensitive zones and the Hirpora Wildlife Sanctuary. At around 600 meters into the trail, we reached Zrado, followed by a gradual ascent to Baliwal. The trail transitions from forested sections of Shahkoot to more open terrain, and at approximately 6.5 km from the trailhead,we reached the Tsurgul Pass. This pass offers the view to Badgam's Shein Meuin Peak and first major alpine vista, opening up into expansive meadows and panoramic views of the Pir Panjal range, including prominent peaks like Brahmasakli(of Kulgam), Dhakyar(of Shopian), Sunder Top (of Banihal) and more. After crossing the pass, a further 3.5 km leads to the final destination, Rainuer Meadows at around 3185 m ASL. The meadows are vast, lush, and relatively less explored, believed to be the second-largest alpine meadow in Kashmir. The terrain is grassy, interspersed with boulders and occasional seasonal streams. Although the standard route is a circuit trek (Hirpora → Rainuer → Dubjan Bridge → Hirpora), we had to return via the same route due to an injury to one member. During the descent, we were warmly hosted by local nomads at a hut near the trail. Around 15 of us were served noon chai, roti, and fresh homemade butter. Despite offering to pay, the nomads politely refused any compensation, showcasing typical high-altitude hospitality(PS: Nomad's of Diskhal are an exception ).
Trek Grade: Easy to Moderate
Total Distance: 19 kms

Mahadev Peak Basecamp:Lidwas

Route Overview

Originally intended as a one-day summit attempt to Mahadev Peak (3966 m ASL), the plan had to be modified due to road closures in Srinagar caused by a cycle race, resulting in a two-hour delay. We initiated the trek from Scholar’s School, Dhara (2030 m ASL) without any breaks. After the initial stretch, the trail passed Padshah Pal, followed by an approach toward Bobjan, a section known for high bear activity and considered one of the more dangerous segments of the route. Several members reported past bear encounters here. Following multiple stream crossings, the trail entered Wovur Gul (I'm not sure about the name), a steep and energy-intensive climb. After gaining elevation in Wovur Gul, Lidwas became visible at a distance. It took about an hour from this point to reach Lidwas.

Lidwas & Above

Lidwas serves as a seasonal settlement for nomadic communities during the six months of summer. We reached Lidwas around 1:30 PM. Out of 20 members, 5(Faizan, Basit, Aakash, Sharik and Yasoob(me)) decided to push further above Lidwas for better vantage points of Mahadev Peak. We ascended an additional hour, reaching an altitude of approximately 3520 m ASL, which provided unobstructed views of Mahadev Peak and surrounding ridgelines. On the descent back to Lidwas, a wildlife sighting occurred, an animal with a furry tail was initially suspected to be a fox, but was later confirmed via binoculars to be a Himalayan Marmot, a first-time sighting for us.

Return Journey

While descending, we retraced the same route. At Bobjan, a group member narrowly avoided stepping on a green snake, marking another notable wildlife encounter. After exiting Bobjan, the trail gradually eased, allowing us to recover from the more challenging sections.

Trek Grade: Moderate to Difficult
Total Distance: 19.6 kms

Shilsar, My First Alpine Lake

30 July 2023
The Shilsar Lakes trek began from Margan Top (~3700 m ASL), a high-altitude pass connecting Islamabad with Kishtwar, after a 120 km drive from Srinagar. The trail starts to the right of the pass with an initial steep ridge followed by a gradual ascent through alpine terrain. Out of the four Shilsar lakes, we visited only two on this route as the remaining are more accessible from a different approach. We reached Shilsar 1 (~3900 m ASL) at around 12:30 PM with an estimated surface area of 6310 sq. meters (season-dependent), followed by Shilsar 2 (~3920 m ASL) after a 20-minute trek where the we had lunch during rain and hail. The lakes marked my first alpine lake experience, with distant views of Nun and Kun peaks visible from Margan Top. I was the first to descent at around 4:00 PM, and had a cup of tea at a local dhaba owned by Majid, and departed from Margan Top at around 5:30 PM.

Trek Grade: Easy to Moderate
Total Distance: 11 kms

The Arshan Valley

20 August 2023
The trek to Arshan Lake began after a ~110 km drive from Srinagar to Sinthan Top (few kilometers before Sinthan Top), a high-altitude pass in South Kashmir. From there, the trail led into Arshan Valley, and after a few hours of trekking, we reached Arshan Sar (also known as Drinyan Sar). The route then continued via a steep, rocky ascent to Drinyansar Pass (~4170 m ASL), which offers panoramic views of Zednag, Nehnag, and the Famber Valley. A sharp descent from the pass led to Zednag followed by Nehnag, a distinctive kidney-shaped alpine lake. I further explored the region by ascending to Hoksar 1 (Abdullah Sar on some maps) before descending back to Zednag. We then retraced the trail back over Drinyansar Pass, closely sighting a Himalayan Marmot and reached Sinthan Top by sunset, completing a high-altitude circuit through a rugged and scenic section of Sinthan.

Trek Grade: Moderate to Difficult
Total Distance: 14 kms

Margan Lakes: Choharnag & Khellannag

03 September 2023
The Choharnag trek began with a 120 km drive from Srinagar to Margan Top, after which the trail started towards the left of the pass. A steep ridge was crossed within the first hour, and after approximately 4 km of trekking, the group reached Choharnag 1, part of a cluster of four alpine lakes collectively named Choharnag (meaning "four springs"). Known as one of the easiest alpine lake treks in the region, the route continued to Choharnag 2, the largest of the four, followed by Choharnag 3, which I consider the most scenic amoung the four. Then, Choharnag 4, was reached before ascending a steep 200-meter ridge to Khellan Top (~4300 m ASL), a vantage point offering expansive views of Khellan Nag Lake, whose deep blue color was attributed to mineral content and sunlight angle. From this high point, Shilsar 1 and 2 were also visible through binoculars, previously visited on a trek. Lakes Choharnag 1, 2, and 3 were clearly visible from the ridge. After lunch at Khellan Top, we descended back to Margan Top and concluded the day with tea at Majid’s dhaba, also visited during the Shilsar trek.

Trek Grade: Easy to Moderate(for Choharnag) & Moderate to Difficult (for Khellannag)
Total Distance: 18 kms
